Consider the following three relations in a relational database.

$\text{Employee} (\underline{\text{eId}},\text{Name}), \; \text{Brand}(\underline{\text{bId}},\text{bName}), \; \text{Own}(\underline{\text{eId}},\underline{\text{bId}})$

Which of the following relational algebra expressions return the set of $\textit{elds}$ who own all the brands?

  1. $ \Pi_{eId} \left(\Pi_{eId,bId} (Own) / \Pi_{bId} (Brand) \right)$
  2. $ \Pi_{eId} (Own) – \Pi_{eId} \left( ( \Pi_{eId} (Own) \times \Pi_{bId} (Brand)) – \Pi_{eId,bId} (Own)\right)$
  3. $ \Pi_{eId} \left(\Pi_{eId,bId} (Own) / \Pi_{bId} (Own) \right)$
  4. $ \Pi_{eId} \left( (\Pi_{eId}(Own) \times \Pi_{bId} (Own)) / \Pi_{bId} (Brand) \right)$

Option A-It will give eid which will pair with every bid of brand so this will give correct answer.

Option B-In this option first there is a cross product between eid of Own and bid of BRAND so it will give all combination of eid and bid then there is a set difference between eid,bid of Own table this will give all employees which does not own all brand.Then at last there is set difference with eid of Own,so it will give eid which pair with every bid of brand (eid which owns all brand)

Option C-it will give eid which pair with every bid of Own (not brand) so this will give wrong answer.

Option d- 

Suppose there is an instance of Own(eid,bid) like this (e1,b1) (e1,b2) (e2,b1) and suppose in brand there are only two bid b1 and b2 so it should give e1 as answer but according to option d there will be cross product between eid of Own and bid of Own so after cross product it will look like this (e1,b1), (e1,b2),(e2,b1),(e2,b2) and after performing division with bid of brand it will give eid e1 and e2 as answer which is wrong.

So this is incorrect

option A and option B are correct.

Employee(eid, Name), Brand(bid, bName), Own(eid,bid)

set of eids who owns all brands

M1) Πeid[own/Πbid(Brand)] option A

M2) Employees who own some brand - Employees who do not own all brands Option B

Suppose A = (Employees who own some brand x bids of all brands) : All pairings of Emps with atleast 1 brand to all brands possible

B = Πeid[A - Pairs of Employees & the brands they own] :
Eids of emps who do not own all brands (eids of emps who own all brands will be erased)

Πeid(own) - B : Eids of emps who own all brands

Thus Answer is option A & B

Final Answer: {E1, E3} ✅ Correct!

Option (B): π<sub>eId</sub>(Own) − π<sub>eId</sub>((π<sub>eId</sub>(Own) × π<sub>bId</sub>(Brand)) − π<sub>eId,bId</sub>(Own))

Cross product = every employee paired with every brand

E2B2
E3B1
E3B2

Step 4: Subtract actual Own: (Step 3) − π<sub>eId,bId</sub>(Own) = ?

"What's in cross product but NOT in Own?" = Missing pairs

eIdbId
E2B2

Step 5: π<sub>eId</sub>(Step 4) = ?

eId
E2

This is "employees missing at least one brand"

Step 6: π<sub>eId</sub>(Own) − Step 5 = ?

{E1, E2, E3} − {E2} = {E1, E3}

Final Answer: {E1, E3} ✅ Correct!

Option (C): π<sub>eId</sub>(π<sub>eId,bId</sub>(Own) / π<sub>bId</sub>(Own))

Step 1: π<sub>bId</sub>(Own) = ?

bId
B1
B2

This looks the same as π<sub>bId</sub>(Brand)...

But what's the problem?

This divides by brands that appear in Own, not all brands in Brand.

If there's a brand B3 in Brand that nobody owns:

  • π<sub>bId</sub>(Brand) = {B1, B2, B3}
  • π<sub>bId</sub>(Own) = {B1, B2}

These would give different results!

In our example: They happen to be same, so let's assume Brand has B3 that nobody owns:

Brand:

bIdbName
B1Nike
B2Adidas
B3Puma

Now:

  • Option (A) asks: "Who owns ALL brands including B3?" → Nobody!
  • Option (C) asks: "Who owns all brands that appear in Own?" → {E1, E3}

These are different questions!

Option (C) doesn't correctly find "employees who own ALL brands"

Final Answer: Incorrect ❌

Option (D): π<sub>eId</sub>((π<sub>eId</sub>(Own) × π<sub>bId</sub>(Own)) / π<sub>bId</sub>(Brand))

Step 1: π<sub>eId</sub>(Own) = {E1, E2, E3}

Step 2: π<sub>bId</sub>(Own) = {B1, B2}

Step 3: π<sub>eId</sub>(Own) × π<sub>bId</sub>(Own) = ?

eIdbId
E1B1
E1B2
E2B1
E2B2
E3B1
E3B2

This is all possible combinations, not actual ownership!

Step 4: Divide by π<sub>bId</sub>(Brand) = {B1, B2}

Every employee appears with every brand in the cross product, so:

Result = {E1, E2, E3}

Final Answer: {E1, E2, E3} ❌ Wrong!

E2 doesn't own all brands, but this query returns E2!

Answer: (A) and (B) ✅
