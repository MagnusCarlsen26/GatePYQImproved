 Statement 1: \( L_1 = L_2 \iff L_1 \cap \overline{L_2} = \varnothing \)

 Counterexample
Let:
- \( L_1 = (a+b)^* \) (the universal language, regular),
- \( L_2 = \emptyset \) (the empty language, regular).

Then:
- \( L_1 \cap \overline{L_2} = (a+b)^* \cap \Sigma^* = (a+b)^* \neq \varnothing \), so \( L_1 \cap \overline{L_2} \neq \varnothing \).
- Clearly, \( L_1 \neq L_2 \).

Thus, the equivalence \( L_1 = L_2 \iff L_1 \cap \overline{L_2} = \varnothing \) does **not** hold in this case.
This statement is False

Statement 2: \( L_1 \cup L_3 \) is not regular

Counterexample
Let:
- \( L_1 = (a+b)^* \) (the universal language, regular),
- \( L_3 \) be any non-regular language, e.g., \( \{a^n b^n \mid n \geq 0\} \).

Then:
- \( L_1 \cup L_3 = (a+b)^* \), which is regular (since \( L_1 \) already contains all possible strings).

Thus, \( L_1 \cup L_3 \) can be regular even if \( L_3 \) is not regular.
This statement is False

Statement 3: \( \overline{L_3} \) is not regular

Reasoning
Let \( L_3 = \{a^n b^n \mid n \geq 0\} \), which is a non-regular language. Its complement:
- \( \overline{L_3} = \Sigma^* \setminus \{a^n b^n \mid n \geq 0\} \), where \( \Sigma = \{a, b\} \).

Since \( L_3 \) is non-regular, its complement \( \overline{L_3} \) is also non-regular. The class of regular languages is closed under complementation, but \( L_3 \) is not a member of this class.


This statement is True.

 Statement 3:\( \overline{L_1} \cup \overline{L_2} \) is Regular

 Proof:
1. Complementation:  
   Regular languages are closed under complementation.  
   If \( L_1 \) and \( L_2 \) are regular, then \( \overline{L_1} \) and \( \overline{L_2} \) are also regular.

2. Union  
   Regular languages are closed under union.  
   If \( \overline{L_1} \) and \( \overline{L_2} \) are regular, then \( \overline{L_1} \cup \overline{L_2} \) is regular.

 Conclusion
\[
\overline{L_1} \cup \overline{L_2} \text{ is regular.}
\]
 

Final Answer Summary
1. \( L_1 = L_2 \iff L_1 \cap \overline{L_2} = \varnothing \): False.
2. \( L_1 \cup L_3 \) is not regular: False.
3. \( \overline{L_3} \) is not regular: True.
4. \( \overline{L_1} \cup \overline{L_2} \) is regular: True.

  • $L_1, L_2$ are regular languages

  • $L_3$ is not regular

 

Option A:

$$
L_1 = L_2 \iff L_1 \cap \overline{L_2} = \varnothing
$$

The condition $L_1 \cap \overline{L_2} = \varnothing$ implies $L_1 \subseteq L_2$, but equality requires both $L_1 \subseteq L_2$ and $L_2 \subseteq L_1$.

Counterexample:
Let $L_1 = {a}$ and $L_2 = {a, b}$.
Then $L_1 \cap \overline{L_2} = \varnothing$, but $L_1 \neq L_2$.

Hence, Option A is false.

 

Option B:

$$
L_1 \cup L_3 \text{ is not regular}
$$

Although $L_3$ is not regular, the union of a regular language with a non-regular one may still be regular.

Counterexample:
Let $L_1 = \Sigma^*$ (regular) and $L_3 = { a^n b^n \mid n \ge 0 }$ (non-regular). Then $L_1 \cup L_3 = \Sigma^*$, which is regular.

Hence, Option B is false.

 

Option C:

$$
\overline{L_3} \text{ is not regular}
$$

If we assume $\overline{L_3}$ is regular, then since regular languages are closed under complement, $L_3 = \overline{\overline{L_3}}$ would also be regular, contradicting the given condition.

Hence, Option C is true.

 

Option D:

$$
\overline{L_1} \cup \overline{L_2} \text{ is regular}
$$

Regular languages are closed under complement and union. Since $L_1$ and $L_2$ are regular, their complements $\overline{L_1}$ and $\overline{L_2}$ are also regular.
Therefore, $\overline{L_1} \cup \overline{L_2}$ is regular.

Hence, Option D is true.

$$
\boxed{\text{C and D are always true.}}
$$
